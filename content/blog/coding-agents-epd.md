## Meta Description
Coding agents didn't just speed up development. They changed the whole game for EPD teams. Here's what that actually means for your role and your team.

## Introduction

Everyone thought AI coding tools would just make engineers faster. Write code quicker, ship features sooner, done.

That's not what happened.

When code becomes cheap to produce, the whole structure of how product, design, and engineering teams work starts to break. Not a little. Fundamentally. The bottleneck moved. And if you haven't noticed yet, you will soon.

---

## The Old Workflow Is Gone

For years, EPD teams ran the same playbook. Product writes requirements. Design builds mockups. Engineering implements. Repeat.

That workflow existed for a reason. Implementation was slow and expensive. Specialists were necessary because the cost of a wrong decision was measured in weeks of engineering time. So you over-documented. You handed off specs. You created elaborate approval chains.

Coding agents changed the math. A prototype that used to take two weeks of engineering time now takes a few hours. Sometimes less.

So the entire reason that waterfall workflow existed? Gone.

### What Replaces It

Prototypes are the new PRDs.

Instead of writing a detailed requirements doc and waiting for design, you build a rough working version. That prototype becomes the thing everyone reacts to. It's the conversation. It's the spec.

The old spec-first model doesn't make sense anymore. By the time you finish a 20-page PRD, you could've built three versions of the thing and tested them.

---

## The New Bottleneck: Review, Not Code

Here's where it gets interesting. Execution is cheap now. So what's the constraint?

Review.

Every prototype needs someone to check if it's architecturally sound (engineering), solves the actual problem (product), and feels right to use (design). But you're generating way more prototypes than before. Review workload went up. People doing reviews stayed the same.

This is the real shift. Shipping speed is now gated by how fast your team can review, validate, and decide. Not by how fast you can write code.

Teams that figure this out will move faster. Teams that don't will drown in a backlog of half-baked prototypes waiting for sign-off.

### Documents Still Matter, Just Differently

Before: you wrote a PRD to explain what to build.

Now: you write a short intent document to help reviewers understand why you built what you built.

Big difference. One is a directive. One is context. Reviewers can't tell if a weird edge case in your code is intentional or a mistake without some explanation of your intent. That's what the document does now.

Some teams are experimenting with using the actual prompts they gave the coding agent as a kind of specification. Versioned prompts that show exactly what was asked for. It's early, but it makes sense.

---

## Generalists Are Winning

If you've been a generalist, someone who can move across product, design, and engineering without being world-class in any one area, this is your moment.

Here's why. Communication overhead is expensive. A team of three specialists (one product, one design, one engineer) has to coordinate constantly. Every handoff introduces lag, lost context, and translation errors.

One person who can do all three, using coding agents to cover the execution gap, can move faster than that team. Not a little faster. Dramatically faster.

"Communication is the hardest part of everything." One person who can do product, design, and engineering will outrun a team of three because they skip the coordination tax entirely.

That used to be true in theory. Now it's true in practice because the execution barrier is gone.

---

## Two Types of People in the Agent Era

Teams are splitting into two archetypes. Not officially. But effectively.

**Builders** can take an idea to a working prototype. They have baseline product intuition, can direct a coding agent, and have enough design sense to know when something feels off. They move fast. They make decisions autonomously. Give them guardrails like a component library and a test suite, and they'll ship.

**Reviewers** are systems experts who evaluate what builders produce. Is the architecture sound? Does this actually solve the problem? Is the interaction right? They need deep expertise and they need to work fast. The bar is high.

And here's the thing. These aren't permanent roles. An engineer can move toward being more of a builder by developing product and design instincts. Or they can go deeper on systems expertise to become a better reviewer. Same for product and design people.

The tracks are opening up. Where you started matters less than where you're heading.

---

## The Skill That Actually Matters Now

When anyone can execute quickly, what separates good outcomes from wasted effort?

System thinking.

Not just for engineers. For everyone.

For engineers, that means having a real mental model of architecture, API design, and how systems interact. For product people, it means accurately understanding what users actually need, not what they say they need. For designers, it means knowing why certain interfaces work and others don't.

Product sense matters too. Coding agents will build exactly what you tell them to build. If you have bad product judgment, you'll direct them to build the wrong things. And doing that fast just creates more waste. More prototypes nobody wanted. More review work for your teammates.

"Good product thinking is more valuable than ever. Bad product thinking is more wasteful than ever."

That's the sharpest version of it. Agents amplify your judgment, good or bad.

---

## What This Means for Your Team Right Now

If you're leading an engineering, product, or design function, a few things are worth looking at.

**Prototype review is probably your bottleneck.** If ideas are moving fast but shipping is slow, the problem isn't execution. It's review. You need faster, lighter-weight review processes that don't sacrifice quality.

**Your best builders need guardrails.** A shared component library, a test suite, a design system. These aren't nice-to-haves. They're what let builders move fast without creating chaos that reviewers have to clean up.

**Hiring for background alone doesn't work anymore.** Someone from a design background who thinks systemically and has good product judgment can outperform a senior engineer who can't direct an agent effectively. Hire for curiosity and thinking quality, not just credentials.

**Specialists still matter.** But the threshold is higher. Being solid in one domain isn't enough. The valuable specialists are the ones who are world-class in their area, fast at reviewing, and can work alongside agents.

---

## The Real Takeaway

The teams treating coding agents as just a productivity boost are missing the bigger shift. It's not about writing code faster. It's that execution is no longer the constraint.

The constraint is judgment. Knowing what to build, how to review it well, and how to move fast without producing junk.

If you're a business thinking about how to build or improve your web presence, this shift matters to you too. The agencies and freelancers you work with are either adapting to this or they're not. The ones who are will move faster and deliver more for the same budget.
