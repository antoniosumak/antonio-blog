Six practical design tips that turn generic websites into memorable ones. No design degree needed, just smart choices around typography, visual hierarchy, and depth.

## Introduction

Here's something we see all the time. A business spends real money on a website, and it comes out looking like every other website. Clean? Sure. Professional? Technically. But memorable? Not even close.

The problem isn't that the design is bad. It's that it's safe. Predictable choices, default fonts, flat layouts. Nothing wrong with any of it individually, but together it all blends into background noise.

Your website is often the first and only impression someone has of your brand. If it doesn't make them feel something, they won't remember you.

So here are six concrete techniques that take a site from forgettable to genuinely impressive. No design degree required.

---

## Start With Typography (It's Your Biggest Quick Win)

Most people pick a font and move on. That's fine, but it's also why most websites look the same.

There's a smarter approach. Think of font usage in three levels.

**Level 1** is using one font everywhere. Safe, basic, gets the job done.

**Level 2** is using super families. These are large font families that include sans-serif, serif, and sometimes monospace versions, all designed to work together. You get variety without fonts clashing.

**Level 3** is where things get interesting. You pair fonts from completely different foundries that just work together. Most personality and visual interest comes from this.

### The Anchor Font Method

Here's a trick that flips the usual process. Instead of starting with the body font and building up, start with the headline font first.

Why? The headline is the first thing anyone sees on your page. It sets the entire personality. Is the brand modern? Technical? Warm? That decision starts with the headline.

Once you've got your anchor headline font, every other font you add should:

- Feel like it came from the same world
- Add enough contrast to be visually interesting
- Not be so similar that it looks like a mistake (Georgia next to Times New Roman just feels like a bug)

Quick tip: check out FontsInUse.com. Search for your headline font and you'll see what other designers have paired it with in real projects. Removes all the guesswork.

## Every Great Website Has a "Star of the Show"

Think about the last website that actually stuck with you. Chances are there was one thing that grabbed your attention right away. A striking image, a bold font treatment, an animation, a 3D element. Something.

That's the star of the show. The single visual element that makes people stop scrolling and pay attention.

But here's where most people get it wrong. They pick something cool that has zero connection to what the business actually does. A cool visual with no meaning behind it feels hollow.

### Make Your Star Mean Something

Brandon Sanderson starts every book with what he calls a "seed," one core "what if" question that the entire story grows from.

Your star works the same way. It should be the visual expression of what the product or brand actually does. Not random. Not just pretty. Connected.

Say you're building a site for a SaaS company that turns complex data into simple dashboards. Your star could be an abstract image that looks like a stylized graph. It references the product visually while still looking striking. Everything else on the page grows from that one element.

## Visual Rhyming Ties Everything Together

This one's subtle but it hits different when you get it right. Visual rhyming means repeating small design details across the entire site so everything feels like it belongs to the same universe.

What can rhyme? Shapes, colors, textures, motifs. Take elements from your star and echo them everywhere.

Say your logo has triangles in it. You could:

- Swap generic plus icons for triangle-shaped dropdown arrows
- Add a triangle arrow to your main CTA button
- Use triangle shapes as masks over images
- Build callout boxes with triangular accents

Now every element on the page feels intentional. Like it was designed as one cohesive thing, not assembled from random parts.

This is the difference between a website that looks "nice" and one that looks like a real designer spent time on it.

## Add Depth (But Keep It Quiet)

A lot of standout websites make you feel like you're walking into them rather than just scrolling past them. That feeling comes from depth.

Ways to add it:

- **Textures on backgrounds.** Noise is popular right now and it works.
- **Glass or frosted glass effects** on cards and navigation elements.
- **Layering elements** so they feel stacked in physical space, not flat on a screen.

But here's the critical rule: depth has to stay subtle. It should support your star of the show, never compete with it. Think of it like a well-designed room. You notice the texture on the walls and the materials on the furniture, but they're not screaming for your attention.

If your depth effects are the first thing someone notices, you've gone too far.

## Use Text Opacity to Create Instant Hierarchy

This takes about 30 seconds to apply and makes a surprising difference.

Google's Material Design system doesn't use 100% opacity on all text. They use roughly 87% for high-emphasis text and 60% for medium-emphasis text.

You don't need to copy those exact numbers. But the principle is gold.

Keep your headlines at full opacity. Drop supporting text to around 60-70%. That's it. Your eye immediately knows what to read first, what to skim, and what to skip.

It's one of those changes that seems tiny but makes the whole page feel intentional. Like someone actually thought about the reading experience instead of just dumping text on a page.

## Iterate Radically, Not Incrementally

This last one isn't a visual technique. It's a mindset shift.

Music producers do this thing where they take an artist's song and force extreme variations. Speed it way up. Slow it way down. Sing an octave higher. Change the key entirely. It breaks them out of their original thinking and toward the best version.

Apply the same thing to your website. Don't just nudge your first draft. Try something completely different.

- Flip from dark mode to light mode
- Change the layout entirely
- Try a different star of the show
- Reverse your color scheme

One redesign project went through 12 different versions of the same hero element. The first version was fine. Good, even. But version 12 was on a completely different level. That only happened because they kept pushing past "good enough."

Your first idea is a starting point, not the finish line.

---

## Wrapping Up

Six tips. None of them require a design degree or expensive tools. Typography with intention, a meaningful star element, visual rhyming, subtle depth, text opacity for hierarchy, and radical iteration.

The difference between a forgettable website and one that builds trust usually isn't one big flashy thing. It's a bunch of small, intentional choices that work together.

If your website looks like every other website in your industry, it's not doing its job. And that's costing you more than you think.
